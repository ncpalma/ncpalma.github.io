About Me

I hold a Ph.D. in Political Science from Pontificia Universidad Católica de Chile, and I am currently a postdoctoral researcher at COES – Centro de Estudios de Conflicto y Cohesión Social. My research focuses on electoral behavior, institutional reforms, and political disaffection, particularly in the Chilean context.

My doctoral dissertation, titled “Democratic Evolution in Chile: Electoral Reforms, Voter Behavior, and Democratic Satisfaction”, is composed of three articles:

  • Article 1: Investigates how voting for the winning option in the 2020 Chilean plebiscite affected satisfaction with democracy. Using panel data and a difference-in-differences approach, it shows that winning increased satisfaction in both the short and long term.
  • Article 2: Analyzes the role of political disaffection and disinterest in voting for outsider candidates in Chile’s 2021 presidential election. It finds that Gabriel Boric, a reformist outsider, attracted disaffected voters more than his populist counterparts.
  • Article 3: Examines the impact of electoral reform and increased district magnitude on voter turnout. The study reveals a U-shaped effect: turnout declined in formerly competitive districts and increased in previously less competitive ones.

My research explores how institutional and social changes influence electoral participation, voter satisfaction, and democratic legitimacy. I use a mix of quantitative methods, including panel data analysis, difference-in-differences designs, and multinomial logistic regression models applied to original and secondary survey data such as ELSOC.
